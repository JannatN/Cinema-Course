Introduction

Cinema is a contemporary art, as it joined the rest of the arts at the end of the nineteenth century, and was also considered an expressionist art. The motion picture was one of its first tools, followed by sound. Cinema started out as just a "game", but some considered it just entertainment, the noise of which faded after a short time. However, the cinema reflected the look of the pessimists, as it made its way and became the eye of the artist who watched and enthusiastically. With the passage of time, its functions multiply and transform from mere entertainment to a more mature art, and thus acquire a language of its own, derived from the diversity of its different subjects in different schools, the complexity of its concepts, and the questions it poses about its identity and affiliations to different peoples in its intellectual and cultural ideology.

Arab Mena region cinema

The cinema of Egypt, the Maghreb (Algeria, Tunisia, Morocco), Syria, Iraq, Lebanon, Bahrain, Emirate, Kuwait, Jordan, Libya, Saudi Arabia, and Palestine are all considered as Arab Mena region cinema. The art of cinema reached the Arab countries at the beginning of the last century and arrived in Egypt at the end of the twenties, that is, after nearly thirty years of global cinematic experiences in both Europe and America to the former Soviet Union.

Over the following century, only seven Arab states established a significant film production activity. During this period Egypt, the cultural center of the Arab world, produced almost 75 percent of the total output of films in the region as well as comprising the largest share of the Arab film market. Eventually, Cairo became—and in many respects remains—the region's main center for film studios, artists, training facilities, technical support and expertise, and distribution networks. However, since the 1950s (and particularly since the mid-1980s) filmmaking activity in Syria, Lebanon, the Palestinian community, Tunisia, Morocco, and Algeria, as well as in Arab immigrant centers, has led to an increasingly diverse and progressively more interactive Arab film culture.

Arab cinema and hollywood

Before considering Arab cinema itself, it is useful to note a critical dynamic that has consistently marred Arab people's relationship with films: their image in Western cinemas. Many Arabs and Arab filmmakers view the portrayal of the Arab world in the West as a major obstacle to screening, publicizing, and appreciating a fundamentally vibrant Arab film culture. Hollywood in particular has played a consistent role in spreading images that inculcate racist attitudes toward Arabs.

Since 1896, Hollywood filmmakers have categorized "the Arab" as the enemy. In Hollywood films the image of the Arab is all too familiar: dark-skinned men with large noses and black beards, wearing keffiyehs (headscarves) and dark sunglasses, and in the background a limousine, women in a harem, oil wells, and camels. A variation on this stereotype is the man with a gun in hand and hatred in his eyes uttering "Allah" or incomprehensible words. Arab women are mostly silent and ugly, or beautiful belly dancers and slaves who are often vindictive.

In hundreds of Hollywood films, Arabs are the bad guys, and the good guys are out to eliminate them. For example, Emory Johnson in The Gift Girl (1917), Gary Cooper in Beau Sabreur (1928). Long before September 11, 2001, Hollywood Arabs have been invading America and killing its innocents. From The Golden Hands of Kurigal (1949) to The Terror Squad(1987) to The Siege (1998), the theme of the looming Arab threat to America persists.

The extent to which this stereotypical image of Arabs and the Arab world has influenced Western attitudes toward Arab cinema itself is a subject for further discussion. Since the 1990s, Western interest in films originating in Arab countries has increased. More than ever before, Arab films are making the rounds of film festivals and repertory or art cinemas in Europe and North America.
